# Visualization Patterns

> Reference for Phase 2-4. Load when analyzing content and proposing approaches.
> For full brand specs, read `design-kit/design-tokens.md` and `design-kit/brand-identity.md`.

---

## 1. Output Mode Taxonomy

Three fundamentally different output types. Deciding the mode BEFORE designing determines everything: what you extract, how you propose, what style you use.

### Information Graphic

- **When:** Content's strongest layer is Layer 4 (concrete data, frameworks, comparisons). Viewer should LEARN something.
- **Looks like:** Scorecards, numbered steps, comparison grids, card grids, funnels. Scans in 1 second.
- **Text density:** Moderate. Structured labels, short descriptions, specific data.
- **Style:** Always Unstuck brand system — deep purple (#463187) signature, charcoal black (#2C262E), orange (#FF6719) single accent, lavender (#E0D6FF) and peach (#FFE8DC) fills. Alfa Slab One headlines, Schoolbell body. Film grain texture on all backgrounds.

### Editorial Illustration

- **When:** Content's strongest layer is Layer 3 (opinions, claims, beliefs). Viewer should FEEL something.
- **Looks like:** Cinematic scenes, physical metaphors, spatial relationships. The scene IS the message.
- **Text density:** Minimal. One powerful line. The image communicates.
- **Style:** Discovered from emotional register + style anchors (see section 3), but ALWAYS grounded in the Unstuck color palette — no invented colors.

### Hybrid (Data-Led Default)

- **When:** Both Layer 3 and Layer 4 are strong.
- **Default:** Data-led hybrid — Information Graphic with Layer 3 claim as the headline/hook.
- **Only go editorial-led** if the claim is dramatically stronger than the data.

## 2. Layout Archetypes for Information Graphics

Six proven structures. Choose based on data shape, not preference. All use the Unstuck brand system.

### Card Grid

**Use when:** Multiple items of equal importance, no sequential order. Tips, benefits, features.

- 2x2 or 3-column grid. All cards same size.
- Each card: number or icon + title (Alfa Slab One) + 1-2 sentence explanation (Schoolbell).
- Card backgrounds alternate between lavender (#E0D6FF) and peach (#FFE8DC).
- Hard purple shadows on every card (4px offset, solid #463187, not blurred).
- ONE card gets an orange (#FF6719) accent element — the most important one.

**Example:** "4 reasons AI social selling works" — 4 equal cards, one reason each.

### Vertical Flow

**Use when:** Sequential process, steps, timeline, anything with natural order.

- Steps flow top to bottom. Clear numbering (1, 2, 3) in Alfa Slab One.
- Each step: its own row with number circle (purple background, white text) + title + brief Schoolbell description.
- Thin purple vertical line connecting steps.
- Max 6 steps — more than that, split into a series.

**Example:** "How to build an AI outreach workflow in 5 steps"

### Comparison

**Use when:** Before/after, this vs. that, good vs. bad. Two sides with a verdict.

- Equal visual weight on both sides. Clear Alfa Slab One labels at top.
- Bold purple vertical divider between sides.
- "Better" side: peach (#FFE8DC) background, warmer feel.
- "Worse" side: lavender (#E0D6FF) background, cooler feel.
- One orange accent on the winning side to draw the eye.

**Example:** "Manual outreach vs. AI outreach — what actually changes"

### Hierarchy (Pyramid / Iceberg / Layers)

**Use when:** Levels of importance, hidden depth, foundational concepts.

- **Pyramid:** Foundation at bottom, goal at peak. Each level labeled in Alfa Slab One. Top level in purple, graduating to lavender at the base.
- **Iceberg:** Small visible portion above surface, large hidden portion below. Purple water line divider. "What people see vs. what it actually takes."
- **Layers:** Different depths with distinct labels. Size = volume or importance.

**Example:** "The LinkedIn iceberg — 1 post visible, 47 touchpoints behind it"

### Funnel

**Use when:** Processes that narrow down, conversion flows, decision sequences.

- Wide at top, narrow at bottom. 3-5 stages max.
- Each stage labeled with Alfa Slab One number or percentage.
- Stage backgrounds alternate lavender and peach.
- Narrowest stage gets the orange accent — the conversion point.

**Example:** "From connection request to signed client — the 5-stage flow"

### Single Hero

**Use when:** One powerful concept deserves full attention. No competing elements.

- One large central visual element (50-60% of canvas).
- Minimal text: Alfa Slab One headline above, 1-2 Schoolbell sentences below.
- The hero number/stat in deep purple, massive.
- Generous breathing room — this is the exception to the "dense" rule.

**Example:** "The one metric that predicts client conversion" — single big number centered

### Stat Row

**Use when:** Key metrics, impressive numbers, data that stops scrolling.

- 3-4 large numbers in a horizontal row.
- Each stat: big Alfa Slab One number in purple + short Schoolbell label below in charcoal.
- Background alternates white and lavender behind each stat.
- One stat gets an orange number — the most impressive one.

**Example:** "213 posts analyzed. 22,565 reactions. 6 post types that work."

## 3. Style Anchors for Editorial Illustration

Only applies to Editorial mode. Information Graphics always use the Unstuck brand system strictly.

For editorial illustrations, the Unstuck palette still applies but the visual treatment shifts based on emotional register:

| Style Anchor | Visual Treatment | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| European editorial / Adult Swim | Moebius linework, vintage French magazine, quiet existential surrealism — rendered in Unstuck purple/black/orange palette | Raw personal truths, contemplative content |
| Graphic novel / comic panel | Bold linework, Herge's clear line style, sequential framing — purple and orange as dominant scene colors | Narrative transformations, before/after |
| Risograph / analog print | Paper grain (amplified from our standard texture), muted palette using lavender/peach/purple, slight registration imperfections | Warm, human, personal stories |
| Minimalist conceptual | Single powerful image, New Yorker cover energy — charcoal black background, one purple element, one orange pop | One strong metaphor, elegant simplicity |
| Vintage science / technical | Scientific illustration, detailed cross-sections — purple ink on white/lavender background, orange annotations | Analytical content, system breakdowns |

Match style to emotional register:

| Content Tone | Style Direction |
|---|---|
| Raw, painful, visceral | European editorial / Adult Swim |
| Reflective, contemplative | Minimalist conceptual |
| Warm, human, imperfect | Risograph / analog |
| Bold, confident, declarative | Graphic novel |
| Analytical, precise | Vintage science |

## 4. How to Propose Correctly

### For Information Graphic — Propose DATA STRUCTURES

Each proposal needs:
1. **The format:** Name the structure (Card Grid, Vertical Flow, Comparison, etc.)
2. **Data mapping:** What specific content goes in which slot
3. **1-second takeaway:** What the viewer LEARNS immediately (a fact, not a feeling)
4. **Why this format:** Why does this structure fit THIS data shape

NEVER propose scenes or metaphors for info graphics. A "hierarchy diagram" is a data structure. A "vast library with glowing books" is a scene. Know the difference.

**Example proposal:**
"Comparison layout — left side shows manual LinkedIn process (6 steps, 3 hours/day) on lavender background, right side shows AI-assisted version (2 steps, 20 minutes/day) on peach background with one orange accent on the time savings number. 1-second takeaway: the time difference is embarrassing. Format fits because the content is explicitly a before/after with two distinct workflows."

### For Editorial Illustration — Propose SCENES

Each proposal needs:
1. **The scene:** What is physically in the image? Objects, space, lighting, composition.
2. **Emotional register:** What does the viewer FEEL? Name the emotion.
3. **Focal composition:** Where does the eye go first?
4. **2-second hit:** Gut reaction before conscious thought — what do they get?

NEVER propose layout types for editorial. Describe a physical place with objects and relationships. Always specify which Unstuck colors dominate the scene.

**Example proposal:**
"Two rooms sharing one wall, rendered in charcoal black with purple shadows. LEFT: cramped, desk drowning in notifications, dim light. RIGHT: same person standing in an open space, one plant, one phone, warm peach-toned light from a window. The wall between has a small crack glowing orange. 2-second hit: I know which room I'm in right now, and I want to be in the other one."
