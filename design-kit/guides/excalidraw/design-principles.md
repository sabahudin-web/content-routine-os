# Visual Design Philosophy

Design thinking for building Excalidraw coaching visuals that communicate, not just display. Adapted to Sabahudin's brand system: purple, orange, charcoal, white.

---

## 0. THE GOLDEN RULE: Illustrate, Don't Decorate

**Every visual is an illustration, not a layout.**

Before placing a single element, close your eyes and imagine: if a brilliant coach were explaining this concept on a whiteboard to someone they cared about, what would they draw? They wouldn't draw a 2x2 grid of cards. They'd draw a big circle shrinking into a small one. They'd sketch a tree branching out. They'd scribble an explosion of arrows radiating from a point. They'd make one thing HUGE and another thing tiny to show scale.

**That's what the visual should look like.**

### The Illustration Mandate

1. **Shape IS meaning.** A large purple circle shrinking to a small dot communicates "decline" without a single word. A cluster of lavender bubbles radiating from a center communicates "explosion of possibilities." Use shape, size, and spatial arrangement to tell the story BEFORE adding any text.

2. **Text is a label, not the content.** If someone can't understand the visual's message from shapes alone, the illustration has failed. Text names things — layout explains them.

3. **Vary elements aggressively.** Use ellipses, diamonds, lines, arrows, size contrasts, opacity differences, spacing, and clustering. A visual with only rectangles is a spreadsheet. Mix shapes intentionally: circles for organic/human concepts, rectangles for structured/concrete things, diamonds for pivots.

4. **Size is your most powerful tool.** The most important thing should be visually dominant — 2-3x the size of supporting elements. Don't make everything the same size.

5. **Spatial relationships carry meaning.** Things close together are related. Things far apart are opposed. Things overlapping are intertwined. Things in a line show sequence. Things scattered show diversity.

6. **Push creative boundaries.** The safe layout is almost always a grid of cards. Push past that. Ask: what visual would make someone say "Oh, I GET it now"? That's the visual.

### The Card Trap

**Cards and grids are junk food of visual design.** Easy, predictable, and they communicate almost nothing beyond "here are some related items."

| If you're about to make... | Stop. Try this instead. |
|---|---|
| A 2x2 grid of features | Hub-and-spoke with the product at center and features radiating outward at different sizes based on importance |
| A row of process boxes with arrows | A winding path, a river, a staircase ascending, or a funnel narrowing |
| Side-by-side comparison cards | Dramatically different sized shapes — a cramped, heavy cluster (before) vs an open, light, expansive layout (after) |
| A list of benefits in boxes | A tree growing from a seed, with branches representing each benefit |
| A timeline of equal cards | A visual journey — small at the start, growing bigger as you progress |

**The only legitimate uses for card grids:** Feature comparison tables. Team member introductions. Pricing tiers. Things that are genuinely homogeneous and equal in importance.

For everything else — **illustrate.**

## 1. Core Philosophy

### Show, Don't Tell

A visual that requires reading is a document, not a learning tool. Every visual should communicate its point through **visual structure** — shapes, spatial relationships, color, and hierarchy — with text serving as labels, not explanations.

**The 3-second glance test:** Can someone look at this visual for 3 seconds and understand the point? If not, the visual design has failed.

### Rich, Not Sparse

Coaching visuals should feel intentional and complete — not wireframes. A visual with a title and three bullet points is a waste of the medium. Use the full visual vocabulary: shapes, arrows, containers, color-coding, spatial grouping.

"Rich" doesn't mean "cluttered." Every element should earn its place.

## 2. Outcome Thinking

Before designing ANY visual, answer these four questions.

### Purpose
> "What does this visual need to COMMUNICATE?"

Not "what information goes here" — what understanding should the learner gain?

### Transformation
> "What changes in the learner's mind after seeing this?"

They go from not knowing to knowing, or confused to clear, or overwhelmed to confident.

### Memory
> "What's the ONE image or pattern they'll remember?"

If someone describes this visual to a colleague, what image will they recall? That's the focal point.

### Action
> "What does this visual make them want to do next?"

This shapes whether the visual ends with a CTA, a cliffhanger, or a resolution.

**Run this for every visual.** It takes 10 seconds and prevents visuals that exist without purpose.

## 3. Creative Ideation: Finding the Shape of Your Idea

Before choosing a diagram type, find the **visual metaphor** that captures the concept's essence.

### The Metaphor Discovery Process

Ask: **"What is this concept LIKE?"**

Don't jump to boxes and arrows. Find the *shape* of the idea:
- Is growth like a **rocket launching**, a **staircase climbing**, or a **snowball rolling**?
- Is complexity like a **tangled rope**, **layers of an onion**, or a **maze**?
- Is transformation like a **caterpillar becoming butterfly**, a **bridge crossing**, or **renovating a house**?

### Concept-to-Visual Metaphor Library

| Abstract Concept | Visual Metaphors | When to Use |
|------------------|------------------|-------------|
| **Growth** | Rocket, staircase, tree growing, mountain climb | Revenue, skills, team size, capability expansion |
| **Connection** | Bridge, web/network, intertwined roots, chain links | Partnerships, integrations, relationships |
| **Transformation** | Metamorphosis, sunrise, renovation, evolution | Before/after, product pivots, culture change |
| **Simplicity** | Single line, decluttered desk, cut diamond | Removing complexity, distilling essence |
| **Protection** | Shield, fortress, umbrella, vault | Security, safety, defense, compliance |
| **Speed** | Lightning, race track, bullet train | Performance, efficiency, quick wins |
| **Foundation** | Pillars, building blocks, deep roots, anchor | Core values, principles, fundamentals |
| **Discovery** | Telescope, map with X, lightbulb moment | Insights, research, aha moments |
| **Journey** | Path/road, compass, map, footsteps | Roadmaps, customer journey, career progression |
| **Bottleneck** | Hourglass, traffic jam, narrow doorway | Constraints, blockers, capacity limits |
| **Integration** | Puzzle pieces, gears meshing, orchestra | Combining systems, team synergy, ecosystems |

### The "Movie Poster" Exercise

Before designing any visual, imagine: **If this concept were a movie, what would the poster look like?**

- "We simplified complex workflows" -> Movie poster: A tangled mess transforming into one clean purple line
- "We connect buyers and sellers" -> Movie poster: A bridge spanning a canyon, people walking across
- "Our platform reduced costs by 40%" -> Movie poster: A heavy weight being lifted off someone's shoulders

That image IS your visual.

### Emotion-to-Visual Style Mapping

| Target Emotion | Visual Style | Elements to Use |
|----------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Excitement** | Dynamic, diagonal, movement | Arrows, trajectories, ascending lines |
| **Trust** | Stable, grounded, symmetric | Balanced layouts, pillars, solid shapes |
| **Urgency** | Sharp, contrasted, bold | Orange accents, narrowing funnels |
| **Calm/Confidence** | Spacious, clean, minimal | Lots of whitespace, lavender fills, simple shapes |
| **Curiosity** | Revealing, layered | Partially hidden elements, peek-behind effects |
| **Empowerment** | Ascending, expansive, open | Upward arrows, breaking through, horizon imagery |

## 4. Visual Vocabulary

Diagram types for coaching visuals. Each has a natural use case — creative remixing is encouraged.

| Diagram Type | Structure | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Hub & Spoke** | Central node + radiating connections | Core concept with related ideas, features, benefits |
| **Pipeline / Flow** | Linear shapes connected by arrows | Processes, workflows, timelines, step-by-step |
| **Vertical Flow** | Steps stacked top-to-bottom | Sequences with descriptions, funnels |
| **Comparison** | Side-by-side panels | Before/after, old/new, problem/solution |
| **Grid / Matrix** | 2x2 or 2x3 grid of equal cards | Features, pricing tiers — ONLY for homogeneous content |
| **Hierarchy / Nested** | Large container with smaller elements inside | Composition, containment, system architecture |
| **Single Concept + Annotation** | One large element with callout arrows | Detailed explanation of one thing |
| **Metrics / Results** | Large numbers as focal point | Impact, outcomes, proof points |
| **Timeline** | Horizontal line with events marked | History, roadmap, milestones |
| **Cycle** | Circular arrangement with arrows | Repeating processes, feedback loops |
| **Mindmap** | Central topic with branching subtopics | Exploring connections, brainstorming, topic overview |
| **Roadmap** | Sequential phases with milestones | Plans, learning paths, project timelines |
| **Blueprint** | Layered system view with connections | Architecture, "how it all works" overviews |

## 5. Quality Standards

### Pre-Generation Checklist

Run this BEFORE generating JSON for each visual:

- [ ] **Purpose clear?** — Can you state what this visual communicates in one sentence?
- [ ] **Diagram type chosen?** — Does the visual structure match the content relationship?
- [ ] **Focal point identified?** — Which element should the eye go to first?
- [ ] **Element count reasonable?** — 5-15 elements per section (not counting the frame)
- [ ] **Text minimal?** — Labels and keywords, not sentences
- [ ] **Colors meaningful?** — Every color choice has a reason (brand semantic or grouping)
- [ ] **Brand applied?** — Purple as primary, orange used once max, lavender for fills
- [ ] **Coaching test?** — Would a learner understand this without narration?

### Good vs. Bad Checklist

| Good | Bad |
|---|---|
| One focal point per visual | Everything the same size/weight |
| Visual structure carries meaning | Text carries meaning, visuals are decoration |
| 2-3 colors per section (from brand palette) | Colors used arbitrarily |
| Parallel elements consistent (same size, style) | Boxes of random sizes |
| Every element connects to something | Orphan elements floating alone |
| Whitespace intentional (breathing room) | Either cramped or empty |
| Text is labels (2-5 words per element) | Text is sentences or paragraphs |
| Learner can follow without narration | Requires explanation to make sense |

## 6. Saba's Brand Color System

### Semantic Palette

| Role | Fill (bg) | Stroke | Use For |
|------|-----------|--------|---------|
| **Primary** | `#E0D6FF` (lavender) | `#463187` (purple) | Main concepts, emphasis, containers |
| **Accent** | `#FFE8DC` (peach) | `#FF6719` (orange) | ONE emphasis point per slide max |
| **Neutral/Structure** | `#FFFFFF` (white) | `#C4B8F0` (light purple) | Structural elements, backgrounds |
| **Canvas/Frame** | `#FFFFFF` (white) | `#C4B8F0` (light purple) | Slide frames |

### Text Colors

| Context | Color |
|---------|-------|
| All text (primary) | `#2C262E` (charcoal) |
| Secondary / muted text | `#8A7A8F` (muted purple-gray) |
| On lavender background | `#463187` (purple) |
| On white background | `#2C262E` (charcoal) |

### Color Rules

- **Purple is the brand anchor** — use it for the most important shapes and stroke colors
- **Orange is the accent** — maximum ONE element per section, only the single most critical point
- **Lavender fills** (`#E0D6FF`) — for primary concept shapes; feels calm and brand-consistent
- **Peach fills** (`#FFE8DC`) — only when orange stroke is used (they're a matched pair)
- **White canvas** — never dark backgrounds, never rainbow palettes
- **75% white/light, 20% purple structure, 5% orange emphasis** — the ratio that makes it look premium

### Color Meaning in Saba's System

| Concept | Color Treatment |
|---------|-----------------|
| Primary/main concept | Purple stroke `#463187` + Lavender fill `#E0D6FF` |
| The key insight / most important thing | Orange stroke `#FF6719` + Peach fill `#FFE8DC` |
| Supporting/structural elements | Light purple stroke `#C4B8F0` + White fill `#FFFFFF` |
| Arrows and connectors | Purple `#463187` or Charcoal `#2C262E` |

Don't use color for decoration. Two shapes of the same color should belong to the same category.

### Color Hierarchy Through Contrast (CRITICAL)

**Parent and child elements MUST use different fill colors.** If a hub uses lavender fill, its children must use white fill. If a container uses white fill, its sub-items use white fill with a lighter stroke. Never give parent and child the same treatment — it flattens the hierarchy and makes the diagram unreadable.

| Level | Stroke | Fill | Example |
|-------|--------|------|---------|
| **Level 1 — Anchor** | `#463187` (purple, thick 3-4px) | `#E0D6FF` (lavender) | Central hub, primary concept |
| **Level 2 — Containers** | `#463187` (purple, normal 2px) | `#FFFFFF` (white) | Command zones, category boxes |
| **Level 3 — Sub-items** | `#C4B8F0` (muted lavender, thin 1px) | `#FFFFFF` (white) | Steps, features, details inside containers |
| **Accent — Key callout** | `#FF6719` (orange, 2px) | `#FFE8DC` (peach) | ONE critical highlight per zone |

**The rule:** Each level DOWN in the hierarchy gets a LIGHTER treatment. Anchor = lavender fill + thick stroke. Containers = white fill + normal stroke. Sub-items = white fill + light stroke. This creates instant visual depth.

**Common mistake to avoid:** Giving containers the same lavender fill as the hub. This makes everything look the same importance level. Containers should always be white when the hub is lavender.

## 7. Visual Hierarchy Rules

Four dimensions that create hierarchy. Use them deliberately.

### Size = Importance

- Most important element is the largest (1.5-2x others)
- Supporting elements proportionally smaller
- Annotations and labels smallest
- Title text: 28-36 -> Label text: 16-20 -> Caption text: 12-14

### Color = Meaning (in Saba's system)

- Purple: primary concepts, "the thing you're explaining"
- Orange: THE critical point, the one thing that must stand out
- Lavender: supporting shapes, context
- Charcoal: all text

### Position = Sequence

- Left to right = time flow, progression, improvement
- Top to bottom = hierarchy, priority, funnel
- Center = focal point, most important
- Periphery = supporting context, details

### Contrast = Focus

- High contrast (dark purple on lavender) = important, look here
- Low contrast (light purple on white) = background, structural
- One element per section should "pop" — the thing the eye finds first
- strokeWidth 2-4 for emphasis, strokeWidth 1 for structure

## 8. Heuristics That Make It Look Good

### One Focal Point Per Visual Section

Every section has ONE thing the eye should go to first. Make it the largest, highest-contrast, or most centrally positioned element.

### Flow Direction Guides the Eye

- **Left to Right** for processes, timelines, improvement
- **Top to Bottom** for hierarchies, funnels, priority
- **Center to Out** for hub-and-spoke, radial concepts
- **Arrows confirm direction** — use them to make flow explicit

### The 3-Second Glance Test

Before finalizing any visual, ask: "If someone glanced at this for 3 seconds, would they understand the point?" If not:
- Increase the focal point's size or contrast
- Reduce the number of competing elements
- Simplify the text to keywords
- Add directional cues (arrows, position)

### Balance: Not Cramped, Not Sparse

- **Too cramped:** Elements touching, no breathing room, text too small
- **Too sparse:** Huge empty areas, one tiny element in a sea of white
- **Just right:** Elements have 20-30px gaps, content fills ~60-70% of the content zone, whitespace is intentional

### Consistency Across Sections

- Same font sizes for the same roles across all sections
- Same color meanings throughout the visual
- Same roughness setting on all elements
- Same card/box sizing for parallel concepts

This makes the visual feel like ONE thing, not a collage.

## 9. Canvas Layout Zones

Each slide is 800x500px. Think of it in zones:

```
+--------------------------------------+
|  TITLE ZONE (y: 20-70)              |
|  fontSize 28-36, purple text         |
+--------------------------------------+
|                                      |
|  CONTENT ZONE (y: 80-420)           |
|  Main visual elements go here        |
|  340px of vertical space             |
|                                      |
+--------------------------------------+
|  FOOTER ZONE (y: 430-490)           |
|  Annotations, sources, CTA           |
+--------------------------------------+
```

For single visuals (mindmaps, roadmaps), the canvas expands as needed — no fixed zones required. Use the full space.
